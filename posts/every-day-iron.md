For some product categories, the consumer offering is hopelessly compromised compared to what professionals use.  I have heard this is true with espresso machines.  I know that it's true with clothing irons and steamers.

### Non-Iron Shirts Are a Lie

Sure, some shirts are just resistant enough to wrinkles that they are wearable fresh out of the dryer.  But even those "non-iron" or "wrinkle-free" shirts tend to look better when ironed, especially when it comes to the collars, plaquets, sleeves, and cuffs.  The bigger issue is that those kinds of shirts are almost always poplin or twill dress shirts,  I have never seen a non-iron oxford cloth button down (OCBD) that looked casual enough to untuck and wear with jeans or chinos.  The classic OCBD you would wear untucked with jeans or chinos is usually 100% cotton and likely outscores every other shirt you own in WPI (wrinkles per inch) when you pull it out of the dryer.

And we haven't even mentioned pants, shorts, and sweaters.

### Irons Are Only Part of the Answer

Reading clothing iron buying guides will teach you that irons that produce more steam work better.  Your $15 legacy brand "Black and Decker" from Walmart, Target, or your nearest hotel room closet fills up with water, eeks out a tiny bit of steam, and maybe gives you a few bigger puffs when you press the steam button.

Rowenta best represents the next tier.  Its irons consistently pump out steam, are built to deal with impurities in your water, and are probably fine for most people who use an iron a few times a year.  But you don't have to read many reviews of these or any other brand of premium clothing irons to find complaints about dripping or leaking.  It doesn't happen all of the time, but every once and a while you're expecting to get a puff of steam and, instead, you end up with globs of water all over the only shirt that you can wear to your meeting that starts in 40 minutes.  It's not every time.  But having this experience, you can't help but feel disappointed that an iron that cost you between $70 to $150 couldn't be designed in a way to prevent this.  And if your iron had any kind of mineral build up, there was always a chance that the water spots would turn into yellowish-brown stains.

### How Do Tailors Deal With This?

Tailors don't deal with this.  I was picking up some altered slacks from a tailor.  While I was waiting, I saw a worker put some slacks into something that looked like a miniature tanning bed.  She losed it like a panini press on the slacks.  I heard a hiss, and then perfectly creased and unwrinkled pants emerged.  I felt like an inhabitant of a primitive alien culture who had been accidentally beamed about the U.S.S. Enterprise.  Am I just supposed to go back now?  Can't Doctor Crusher wipe my memory first?

Starting with a dry cleaning equipment supplier's website, I started looking at what kinds of magical machines were being withheld from the rest of us.  There were heated rollers with a hand crank for flattening bed sheets.  There was a sort of steaming manequin who you dress up in wrinkly clothes and then watch turn into a smaller version of one of those car dealership blow-up tube characters.  And, there were these mirror-polished little boxes with retro-looking light-up switches on them, an iron attached, and Italian brand names.

It seems obvious now, but like every other breakthrough advancement into modernity, the answer lay with pressurized steam.

I started looking into those shiny pressurized steam tanks that took around 12 to 15 minutes to heat up and then would shoot steam out of holes in the attached iron.  I saw that the same manufacturers sold the same shiny box with alternative attachments for jewelers to clean jewelry and even for dentists to clean things that go in peoples' mouths.  This seemed like it could work, but the fit didn't seem quite right.
